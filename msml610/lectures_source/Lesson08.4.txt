* Definition

* Causal Model
- A **causal model** is a series of assignments $\leftarrow$
  - Causality is non-reversible: you can't rearrange the equation

::: columns
:::: {.column width=60%}
- **Example**
  $$
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  & T \leftarrow f_t(u_t) \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$
  where:
  - $u_t, u_y$ are _exogenous_ variables (outside the model)
    - Account for everything not covered by model variables
  - Treatment $T$ and $u_y$ cause outcome $Y$
  - $T$ and $Y$ are _endogenous_ variables (inside the model)

* Applying a Treatment to Sales Example
- Assume the **causal model** is:
  $$
  \small
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  & T \leftarrow f_t(u_t) \\
  & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$
  - Ask _"what would happen to the outcome $Y$ if I were to set the treatment to
    $t_0$?"_
    $$
    \small
    \left\{
    \begin{aligned}
    & T \leftarrow t_0 \\
    & Y \leftarrow f_y(T, u_y) \\
    \end{aligned}
    \right.
    $$
  - The intervention is represented by the $do()$ operator, e.g., $do(T = t_0)$

- Note that **conditioning** and **intervention** are different:
  $$
  \small
  \EE[AmountSold | isOnSales = 1] \ne \EE[AmountSold | do(IsOnSales = 1)]
  $$
  - The **first** is what happens to businesses that make a price cut
    - Some business (e.g., big businesses) are "self-selecting" to cut prices
  - The **second** is what happens if you force every business to engage in sales

* Individual Treatment Effect
- Express the impact of the treatment $T$ on the outcome $Y$ for an individual
  unit $i$
  $$\tau_i = Y_i|do(T=t_1) - Y_i|do(T=t_0)$$
  - The effect $\tau_i$ of going from treatment $t_0$ to $t_1$ for unit $i$ is
    the difference in the outcome of that unit under $t_1$ compared to $t_0$
  - E.g., $AmountSold_i|do(IsOnSales=1) - AmountSold_i|do(IsOnSales=0)$

- You can only observe one term due to the fundamental problem of causal
  inference
  - Represent it in theory, but can't recover it from data

* Potential Outcomes
- Aka "counterfactuals"

- The $do()$ operator represents _"unit $i$ outcome is $Y$ if treatment is set to
  $t$"_
  $$Y_{t,i} \triangleq Y_i | do(T_i = t)$$
  - The first is "Rubin's notation", the second is "Pearl's notation"

- For binary treatment use $Y_{0i}$ (without treatment) vs $Y_{1i}$ (with
  treatment)
  - One is "factual" (i.e., observable)
  - The other is "counterfactual" (i.e., theoretical, not observable)

- There is a slight difference
  - $Y_t = Y|do(T=t)$ is "what a business would sell if it cut prices"
  - $Y|T=t$ is "what a business that cut the prices sold"

* Consistency of the Treatment
- There is a single version of the treatment
  - I.e., $T = T_i$ means only one thing

- E.g.,
  - A drug can come in multiple dosages: only one is used
  - Giving a discount coupon is treated as the value of the coupon is the same,
    it's given only once, in a way that is always equivalent
  - In "help from a financial planner", is "help" a one-time consultation or
    regular advice?

* No Interference
- Aka "Stable Unit of Treatment Value (SUTVA)"
- The effect of one unit is not influenced by the treatment of other units
  - E.g., there are no spillovers or network effects

- E.g., vaccinating one person makes other close people less likely to catch the
  illness even if they don't get the treatment

* Average Treatment Effects
- Assume you can see both potential outcomes $Y_{1i}$ and $Y_{0i}$ at the same
  time (which is impossible)

- **Average Treatment Effect (ATE)**
  - The impact that the treatment $T$ would have on the average:
    $$
    \small
    ATE = \EE[Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}] = \EE[Y | do(T=1)] - \EE[Y | do(T=0)]
    $$
- E.g., average impact of price cuts on amount sold:
  $$
  \small
  ATE = \EE[AmountSold_{1i} - AmountSold_{0i}]
  $$

* Conditional Average Treatment Effects (CATE)
- The impact of the treatment on the units in a group defined by $X=x$
  $$
  \small
  CATE = \EE[(Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}) | X=x]
  $$
- E.g., the impact of having sales during the week of Christmas:
  $$
  \small
  CATE = \EE[AmountSold_{1i} - AmountSold_{0i} | weeksToXmas = 0]
  $$
- Like $ATE$ but on a subgroup with a certain characteristics

* Average Treatment effect on the Treated (ATT)
- The impact of the treatment on the units that got the treatment:
  $$
  \small
  ATT = \EE[(Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}) | T=1]
  $$
- E.g., how much sales increase for the business engaged in price cuts:
  $$
  \small
  ATT = \EE[(AmountSold_{1i} - AmountSold_{0i}) | IsOnSales=1]
  $$

- We can define these quantities $ATE$, $CATE$, $ATT$, but not **measure them**
  directly
  - Because we can't observe both potential outcomes $Y_{1i}$ and $Y_{0i}$ at the
    same time

* Bias
- **Bias** is what makes association different from causation

- The claim _"cutting prices increases the amount sold by a business"_ can be
  questioned with:
  - _"Those businesses that cut prices could probably have sold more anyway, even
    with no price cuts"_

- More formally the argument refers to potential outcomes
  - We are trying to estimate $\EE[Y_{it}] \triangleq \EE[Y_i | do(T=t)]$ using
    $\EE[Y_i | T=t]$ but this is a "biased estimator" (i.e., an incorrect one)

* The Bias Equation
- **Causal inference** is interested in:
  \begingroup
  \small
  $$\EE[Y_1 - Y_0] = \EE[Y | do(T = 1)] - \EE[Y | do(T = 0)]$$
  \endgroup
- **What is measured** is:
  \begingroup
  \small
  $$\EE[Y | T = 1] - \EE[Y | T = 0]$$
  \endgroup

- Let's express one in terms of the other
  - Replacing the observed outcomes (each unit has two potential outcomes but
    only one can be observed and the other is 0)
    $$Y | T=1 = (Y_1 | T=1) + \cancel{(Y_0 | T=1)} = Y_1 | T=1$$
  - Adding and subtracting $\EE[Y_0 | T=1]$, reordering and merging expectations
    \begingroup
    \small
    $$
    \EE[Y | T = 1] - E[Y | T = 0] =
    \EE[Y_1 - Y_0 | T = 1] +
    \underbrace{\EE[Y_0 | T = 1] - \EE[Y_0 | T = 0]}_{\text{Bias}}
    $$
    \endgroup
  - Association = causation + a bias term

//* Decomposing Observed Difference into Treatment Effect and Bias
//
//- Observed Outcomes
//  - Each unit has two potential outcomes but only one is observed:
//
//$$Y | T=1 = Y_1 | T=1 \quad \text{(only treated potential outcome observed)}$$
//
//$$Y | T=0 = Y_0 | T=0 \quad \text{(only control potential outcome observed)}$$
//
//- Step 2: Difference in Means
//
//The observed difference between treated and untreated groups:
//
//$$\mathbb{E}[Y | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y | T=0] = \mathbb{E}[Y_1 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=0]$$
//
//- Step 3: Add and Subtract the Counterfactual
//
//To decompose this into effect and bias, add and subtract $\mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=1]$:
//
//$$\mathbb{E}[Y_1 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=0] = \mathbb{E}[Y_1 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=0] + \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=1]$$
//
//- Step 4: Regroup Terms
//
//Rearrange to group the treatment effect and bias separately:
//
//$$= \mathbb{E}[Y_1 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=1] + \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=0]$$
//
//- Step 5: Merge Expectations
//
//Combine the first two terms using linearity of expectation:
//
//$$= \mathbb{E}[Y_1 - Y_0 | T=1] + \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=0]$$
//
//- Final Decomposition
//
//$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[Y | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y | T=0] = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[Y_1 - Y_0 | T=1]}_{\text{ATT}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_0 | T=0]}_{\text{Bias}}}$$
//
//- Interpretation
//  - **ATT** (Average Treatment Effect on the Treated): The causal effect—what we want to estimate
//  - **Bias**: The difference in control potential outcomes between treated and untreated groups
//    - If bias = 0, then observed difference = causal effect
//    - If bias not 0, observed difference is misleading (confounded)

* The Bias Equation: Remarks
- Association = causation + a bias term
  \begingroup
  \small
  $$
  \EE[Y | T = 1] - E[Y | T = 0] =
  \EE[Y_1 - Y_0 | T = 1] +
  \underbrace{\EE[Y_0 | T = 1] - \EE[Y_0 | T = 0]}_{\text{Bias}}
  $$
  \endgroup

- Bias arises from differences between treated and control groups
  (besides the treatment)
  - Unobservable factors change together with treatment
    - E.g., there are confounders
  - E.g., treated and untreated businesses differ beyond price cuts
    - E.g., differ in size, location, day of the week on which they have a sale,
      cities they are located in

- For association to be causation, treated and control units must be
  **exchangeable**

* Simpson's Paradox: Admission bias in University

- A university has
  - Two departments: Engineering and English
  - Male overall acceptance rate: $\frac{81}{120}$ = 67.5%
  - Female overall acceptance rate: $\frac{58}{120}$ = 48.3%

- Is a bias against females, due to lower acceptance rate?

- Break down data by Engineering and English

\begingroup \scriptsize
| **Department** | **Male** |  |  | **Female** |  |  |
|:-----------|---------:|--:|--:|-----------:|--:|--:|
|            | Applicants | Accepted | Rate | Applicants | Accepted | Rate |
| **University** | 120 | 81 | 67.5% | 120 | 58 | 48.3% |
| **Engineering** | 100 | 80 | 80% | 20 | 18 | 90% |
| **English** | 20 | 1 | 5% | 100 | 40 | 40% |
\endgroup

- At the department level:
  - Women have higher acceptance in Engineering (90% vs 80%)
  - Women have higher acceptance in English (40% vs 5%)

- Paradox reason:
  - Engineering has high acceptance; English has low acceptance
  - More men apply to Engineering; more women apply to English
  - Women apply more to harder department, making their overall acceptance rate
    appear lower, despite higher acceptance in each department

// Identifying the Treatment Effect

* Identifying the Treatment Effect
- We can't observe $\EE[Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}]$ since we can only observe one of the
  potential outcomes $Y_{1i}$ or $Y_{0i}$, but _not both_
  - How can we recover causal quantities from observable data?

- The bias equation says that:
  $$
  \EE[Y | T = 1] - \EE[Y | T = 0] =
  \EE[(Y_1 - Y_0) | T = 1] +
  \underbrace{\EE[Y_0 | T = 1] - \EE[Y_0 | T = 0]}_{\text{Bias}}
  $$
- If $\EE[Y_0|T=0] = \EE[Y_0|T=1]$, i.e., the treatment and the control
  group are comparable (exchangeable) regardless of the treatment, then:
  - The bias term vanishes $bias = 0$
  - Association is causation
  - The measured difference in means equals $ATE = \EE[Y_1 - Y_0]$
  - The measured difference in means equals $ATT = \EE[(Y_1 - Y_0) | T = 1]$$

- **Sales Example**
  - If businesses that do and don't cut prices are similar to each other (i.e.,
    interchangeable), then
  - The difference in amount sold between the ones that cut prices and the ones
    that don't, can be entirely attributed to the price cut

* Exchangeability Property
- The exchangeability property is equivalent to having _potential outcomes_ being
  independent of the treatment:
  $$
  (Y_0, Y_1) \perp T
  $$
  i.e., $\EE[Y_0 | T] = \EE[Y_0]$: the treatment gives no information about the
  potential outcomes (i.e., $\EE[Y_0 | T=1] = \EE[Y_0 | T=0]$)

- **Note**
  - We are not asking for _observed outcome_ $Y$ and treatment $T$ to be
    independent
    - This means that treatment has no effect
  - We are asking for the potential outcome to be independent of the treatment
    - The outcome observed if the subjects received the treatment is independent
      if it received the treatment or not
  - "Knowing whether someone received the treatment tells you nothing about what
    their outcome would have been without it"
  - The treatment is the only thing that causes difference in outcome between
    treatment and control

- **Sales Example**
  - We can't tell apart businesses that chose to engage in sales from those that
    didn't

* Phases of Causal Inference
- A causal inference problem is often broken down into two steps:
  - **Identification**
     - Express the causal quantity of interest in terms of observable data
     - Remove the bias, making treated and untreated comparable, so that all
       the difference can be attributed to the treatment effect
       $$\EE[Y_0 | T=0] = \EE[Y_0 | T=1]$$
     - You need to know how the treatment was distributed or assigned
     - This is why data alone (observational) cannot answer causal questions
  - **Estimation**
     - Use data to estimate the causal quantity of interest:
       $$te = \EE[Y | T=1] - \EE[Y | T=0]$$

* Randomization Removes Bias
- Randomization of treatment forces independence to hold $\implies$ removes bias

- Assume you can randomize the treatment (e.g., you can force a business to cut
  prices)
  $$
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
  & IsOnSales \leftarrow rand(t) \\
  & AmountSold \leftarrow f_y(IsOnSales, u_y) \\
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
  $$
- The dependency from $u_t$ disappears since the assignment mechanism is:
  - Tied to a coin flip (unrelated to other factors in causal mechanism)
  - Fully known
- Treatment becomes independent from potential outcomes

// Chap 2: Randomized Experiments and Stats Review

* A/B Testing Example
- A common strategy among companies is to
  - have a cheap / free product that serves to attract customers
  - Cross-sell other products that are more profitable
    - Use email campaigns to sell premium product
    - When a customer get an email and buys the premium product, they are
      "converted"

- Non-randomized data, you see that:
  $$
  \EE[ Conversion | Email = 1] > \EE[ Conversion | Email = 0]
  $$
  - Problem: marketing team tends to send emails for customers who they think
    they will convert (e.g., using a survey, customers who visited the website
    the most)
  $$
  \EE[ Conversion_0 | Email = 1] > \EE[ Conversion_0 | Email = 0]
  $$
  - I.e., customers would have converted even if the email was not sent

- You need to make the treated and untreated comparable
  - E.g., you can randomize who gets the email

- Imagine that you have 3 emails
  - No email (`no_email`)
  - Long and beautiful email (`long`)
  - Short and to-the-point email (`short`)

- Once you collect the data you get data like:
  ```
  id  gender  cross_sell_email   age    conversion
  --------------
  0
  ```
  - Compute the mean by group in `cross_sell_email`
    ```
    cross_sell_email    conversion
    ------------------------------
    no_email            0.042
    long                0.055
    short               0.125
    ```
  - Compute the ATE, i.e., difference between each treated group and control
    $$ATE = \EE[Y | T=t] - \EE[Y | T=0]$$
    - Sending the short and to-the-point email is best

- You should still do a sanity check that randomization was done right
  - E.g., you can check if `gender` and `age` are balanced in the groups (using a
    statistical test)
  - If not, it might be that the effect is due to sending the email to a specific
    demographic
  - Biases sampling has large effect on small data

* Randomized Controlled Trials
- RCTs are the most reliable way to measure causal effects

- The problem is
  - Very expensive (in time and money)
  - Potentially unethical
  - Sometimes impossible

- You can use an RCT as a benchmark
  - What is the best experiment to answer your question (even if it's not) feasible?
  - Can help discover the causal effect

*
- Standard error of the mean is related to the sample size $n$
  $$
  SE = \frac{\sigma}{n}
  $$

- Imagine you have data about schools in terms of number of students and average
  SAT score
  - An frequent effect is that the top-performing schools have a small number of
    students
    - An explanation is that "fewer students allows teachers to give focused
      attention to each student"

  - In reality
    - Also bottom 1% of schools have few students
    - The effect is due to standard error of the mean: more students means more
      precise estimate and less variation due to chance
    - This is shown by a "triangular" shape of `avg_score` vs
      `number_of_students`

// Chap 3: Graphical Causal Models

*
- Estimate the impact of emails on conversion
  - Cross-sell email = treatment $T$
  - Customer converted to a new product (or not) = outcome $Y$

- $T$ was randomized, so
  - The treatment is independent from the potential outcomes
    $$
    (Y_0, Y_1) \perp T
    $$
  - Thus association equals causation 
    $$
    \EE[Y_1 - Y_0] = \EE[Y | T=1] - \EE[Y | T=0]
    $$

- You can't infer the independence assumption from the data
  - You need to have information to the treatment assignment mechanism (e.g.,
    "emails were randomized")

- Imagine that you believe that `age`, `gender` also cause conversion
  - There are also other variables $U$ (i.e., unobserved) that we didn't measure
    (e.g., customer income, city we are operating in)

* Chains
- In case of $T \to M \to Y$:
  - $M$ is "mediator" since it mediates the relationship between $T$ and $Y$
  - Causation flows only in the direction of the arrows
  - Association flows both ways

- E.g., $Study \to SolveProblems \to JobPromotion$
  - Job promotion is causally dependent on studying
  - The association goes both ways
    - The more you study, the more likely to get a promotion
    - The greater the chances of promotion, the greater your chance of having
      studied (otherwise it would be difficult to get a promotion)
  - So $Study$ and $JobPromotion$ are
    - Dependent or
    - Not independent $Study \cancel \perp JobPromotion$

- If you hold the intermediary variable fixed (i.e., conditioning on $M$, looking
  for only people with the same skills at problem solving), the dependency is
  blocked
  - $T$ and $Y$ are independent given $M$, i.e., $(T \perp Y) | M$
  - $\EE[Y | T, M] = \EE[Y | M]$ and also the inverse $\EE[T | Y, M] = \EE[T | M]$

* Forks

- In a fork $T \leftarrow X \rightarrow Y$:
  - $X$ is a "confounder" (common cause of $T$ and $Y$)
  - Association flows backward through the arrows
  - $T$ and $Y$ are dependent even though neither causes the other: $T \not\perp Y$

- E.g., $CausalKnowledge \leftarrow Statistics \rightarrow MachineLearning$
  - Assume that causal inference doesn't help with machine learning, and vice
    versa, so there is no edge between the 2
  - Knowing causal inference signals statistics knowledge, which predicts ML skill

- E.g., $AnswerQuestion \leftarrow GoodProgrammer \rightarrow GoodEmployee$
  - For this reason in tech interviews you are asked to solve problems that might
    you never find in the job you apply for (e.g., "invert a binary tree")

- Conditioning on the common cause blocks the association:
  $$T \cancel \perp Y, (T \perp Y) | X$$
  - E.g., if you know that you are a good programmer (e.g., you have an
    impressive resume), asking you a tech question won't give new information
    about if you are a good employee or not

* Collider
- In a collider $T \rightarrow X \leftarrow Y$:
  - $X$ is a "collider" (two arrows collide at it)
  - $T$ and $Y$ are independent: $T \perp Y$
  - Conditioning on $X$ opens the path, making $T$ and $Y$ dependent:
    $T \cancel \perp Y | X$

- E.g., $Statistics \rightarrow Promotion \leftarrow FlatteringBoss$
  - There are two ways to get a job promotion
    - You can be good at statistics
    - You can flatter your boss
  - If I don't know if you get or not the promotion (i.e., I don't condition on
    $Promotion$) your level of statistics and flattering are independent
  - If I know you got promoted, and I know you are bad at statistics, then you
    must be good at flattering your boss
    - "Explaining away" since one cause explains the effect, making the other
      cause less likely

- Conditioning on any _descendant_ of the collider opens the path, making the
  variables dependent
  - E.g.,
    $Statistics \rightarrow Promotion \rightarrow Salary \leftarrow FlatteringBoss$:
    conditioning on $Salary$ opens the
    $Statistics \perp FlatteringBoss$ path

* D-separation
- A path is **blocked** given a conditioning set $Z$ if and only if either
  condition holds:
  1. It contains a non-collider that has been conditioned on
  2. It contains a collider that has not been conditioned on and has no descendants
     that have been conditioned on

- Otherwise the path is **open** (nodes can be dependent)

- Two nodes $A$ and $B$ are d-separated (independent) if and only if all the
  paths between them are blocked

* Identification

- It is hard to find the causal effect because treated and untreated are not
  comparable to each other, which results in bias

- Using causal graphs
  - You can understand where the bias is coming from and thus how to neutralize
    it
  - Thus the identification phase becomes equivalent to "close all undesirable
    flows of association"

- E.g., in the consultancy graph
  - There is:
    - A fork on `profits_next_6m`
    - A confounder `profits_prev_6m`
    - A causal path `consultancy` $\to$ `profits_next_6m`
    - A non-causal path (aka "backdoor path") `profits_prev_6m` $\to$
      `profits_next_6m`
  - To close the non-causal flows of association you need to condition on the
    confounder, i.e., `profits_prev_6m`
    - Look at a group of companies with similar past performance
    - Compare the ones that hired consultants (treated) vs the ones who didn't
      (untreated)
    - You are assuming conditional independence, i.e., $(Y_0, Y_1) \perp T | X$

* Backdoor Adjustment
- You are making the "conditional independence assumption", i.e.,
  $(Y_0, Y_1) \perp T | X$

- I.e., units with the same level of covariates $X$ have potential outcomes
  that are the same on average (it's as if they were randomized)

- Closing backdoor paths by conditioning on confounders $X$ makes the causal
  quantity identifiable from observable data (aka "backdoor adjustment")
  $$
  ATE
    = \EE_X[ \EE[Y | T=1] - \EE[Y | T=0 ]
    = \sum_x (\EE[Y | T=1, X=x] - \EE[Y | T=0, X=x]) \Pr(X=x)
  $$
  - ATE is the weighted average of in-group treated-vs-control differences,
    weighted by group size $\Pr(X=x)$

* Confounding Bias

- Confounding happens when there is an open backdoor path through which
  association flows non-causally, because treatment and outcome share a common
  cause

- Example
  - You work in HR and want to know if your new management training program is
    increasing employers' engagement
  - Training is optional
  - Only managers who are already doing a great job attend the program
  - The managers that need the training the most, don't attend
  - When you measure engagement of the teams, engagement under managers who took
    the training is much higher
  - There is a common cause between treatment and outcome, so they move together
    regardless of a causal effect

- You need to close all backdoor paths between treatment $T$ and outcome $Y$,
  i.e., adjust for the common causes / adjust for confounding bias
  - E.g., control for manager's quality before taking the training

- Unfortunately you can't adjust for all common causes
  - Some causes are unknown
  - Known causes might be difficult to measure

* Surrogate Confounding

- The confounder variable might be not be observable / measurable, but you might
  have measured variable that can be proxies for confounder
  - E.g., you can't measure confounder "manager quality"
  - You can measure
    - Causes
      - Manager's tenure
      - Manager's education level
    - Effects
      - Team's attrition
      - Team's past performance

- In this way you don't eliminate bias, but you can reduce it

- Partial remedies when confounders are unobserved:
  - Sensitivity analysis: Instead of asking "did I control for everything?", ask
    "how strong must the unmeasured confounder be to change my conclusion?"
  - Partial identification: Use observational data to place bounds on the causal
    quantity rather than point-identify it
  - Randomization: Intervening on the treatment removes confounding entirely by
    making randomness the sole cause of $T$
